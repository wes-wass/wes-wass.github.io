Experience

Current Position: Research Assistantship
Trinity University Economics Department

I am currently working as a research assistant in the Trinity University Department of Economics. I am currently assisting a faculty member with a project related to Rosenwald schools in the United States. For context, Rosenwald schools were educational facilities opened in the United States during the Jim Crow era that allowed African-American students in the South, and they were frequently the only shot at an education for these students. The professor that I am working with was able to scrape information on every single Rosenwald school, which is incredible within itself, but the data unfortunately didn't come in ready to use (missing county fip codes, county name misspellings, etc.).

I was assigned the task of cleaning the dataset, which primarily focused on fixing county names, where the respective Rosenwald schools resided, that were incorrectly spelled, outdated, or jointed (ie: two counties were listed instead of one). Using Stata, and a mechanism my professor set up to check the dataset for incorrect county names, I was able to pinpoint and fix a significant portion of the incorrect county names. From there, I was able to merge the dataset, with its correct coutny names, to another stylesheet with the counties' fips codes, which was another goal for my professor.

We are soon going to embark on the next stages of this research project. First, we will clean other aspects of the dataset that aren't cohesive, for example, certain schools are listed with no name. Once this is complete, since we have fips codes and GUI data, we will hopefully be able to merge this dataset with other datasets that categorize their data by US county. Then, we can embark on the research process!

Remote Data Analyst
Sharkscope

One of my previous experience positions that is relavant to my future as an economics researcher was my remote data analyst position at Sharkscope, an online poker statistics company.

I was assigned the task of running regressional analysis on a large dataset containing the past 30,000 poker games played on the website at that time. There were 18 variables associated with each individual game (entry), including date, player, player skill, money bet, money won, and more. Our variable of interest, however, was the amount staked in a poker game. My task was to figure out if there were any variables that were significantly correlated with whether a player staked (paid someone else's buy-in in the hopes to take a part of their winnings), and to what extent. I was given a lot of freedom on how I wanted to go about this, so I decided to run both multiple linear regression and difference-of-means t-tests.

I used the R programming language to run these analyses. In the end, I was able to find multiple statistically significant, predictive correlations between various inputs in the dataset and the staking variables. I was never able to satisfy OLS assumptions for causal analysis, but I argue that I was able to find useful trends that would help Sharkscope understand the staking patterns of its clients.
